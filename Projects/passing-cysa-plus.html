THERE ARE NO SHORTCUTS

Now I know you are reading this and thinking no way is that all and you would be 100% correct. There are no shortcuts to knowledge you have to put in the time and effort. What worked for me probably will not work for most people. This is true in anything you do in life you can only try your best to set yourself up for success.

A Little About Me

I want to tell a little story about me and what I am doing right now. I am a recent college graduate that is all around experienced and I would say was doing better then the typical student. I had an internship, certifications, performing well in classes, and winning cyber competitions on a national level.

Yet, I am stuck in a situation most recent graduates are stuck in… this industry is competitive and tough. I can not get interviews or any job offers. I have probably applied to around 200 jobs now and have received 2 interviews, one was with an internal recommendation where I made through 4 rounds of interviews to get beat out by a guy with years of real world experience and projects in cloud detections.

This has made me realize that it is not just recent graduates we are competing with, we are against seasoned professionals that are looking for new opportunities at a bigger company or job role change.

How do we combat this? Honestly…. I have no earthly idea.

The only thing we can do is grow our background, make more projects, practice soft skills, and not fall behind.

Preparing for CySA+

Now back to the meat and taters of this section taking the CySA+. Leading up to it I had just passed another more intense certification GIAC’s ai security automation engineer (GASAE) about 2 weeks ago.

So my mind was tired of studying and a bit burnt out but I had won the CompTIA voucher that was expiring soon from a competition, SummitCTF, a great competition put on by Virginia Tech’s Cyber club.

DO NOT COPY THIS APPROACH BLINDLY

So I was not looking to put forward as much effort in studying as I would most exams. Normally, I would say this is a big NO NO…. Do not treat an opportunity like this, if you are able to put all your effort in.

Now life happens, I personally had a few things leading up to the exam that took precedence but ultimately I could have prepared much better.

Simply put, my tactics were to find a youtube series to study and then take some practice exams.

I did do this partly, but ultimately decided there was no way I would have time to watch a full series on every exam topic.

What I Focused On

So what did I do? I reached out to some people that I know that have already passed the exam and asked what they recommended me focus on.

They said CVSS scoring and at first I was confused… Just like the score itself but then I realized that I didn’t know everything fully that went into the vectors of CVSS and how to read each version.

This was a great tip to study and definitely benefitted me on the exam.

What Actually Helped Me Pass

Now ultimately what led me to passing the exam. I can tell you it wasn’t the practice questions or the studying but the real world experience I have gained in the field. That alone is way more valuable than a study guide.

I was prepared for the unexpected because I have molded myself into a well-rounded professional. I am familiar with offensive and defensive techniques.

I remember sets of questions on there where I may not have known the best way to remediate but I sure do know how to attack it. Then I can take that attack reasoning and work it around to come up with a good defensive measure.

Same with the hands on portions of incident response. I know the attack that occurred so looking at Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) you can easily form an attack chain if you can reason like an attacker.

My Recommendations

I recommend going a step further then just studying and actually gain some sort of applicable knowledge in defense and offensive security.

I recommend HackTheBox this platform is very offensive focused but does also include some blue team Sherlocks for investigations.

I would also recommend knowing everything about proper procedures of handling incidents along with the incident response timeline.

Finally, you should have a solid understanding of widely used security frameworks, including the MITRE ATT&CK Framework and the OWASP Top 10.

  • Understand how to read CVSS vectors and scoring details.
  • Practice reasoning from both an attacker and defender perspective.
  • Learn the proper procedures and timeline for incident response.
  • Develop applicable offensive and defensive security experience.
  • Understand MITRE ATT&CK and the OWASP Top 10.
